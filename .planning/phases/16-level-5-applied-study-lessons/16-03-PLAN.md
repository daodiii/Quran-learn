- Learning Objectives:

- Callout: "Al-Ikhlas was previously used for examples in L1 and L2 lessons. Now analyze the ENTIRE surah, seeing how individual features you learned earlier work together."

**Section 2 — Surah Overview:**
- 4 verses, 15 words, pure theological statement
- GrammarTable: Verse | Arabic | Sentence Type | Key Grammar | Theological Message
- Two halves: V1-2 (affirmation — who Allah IS), V3-4 (negation — who Allah is NOT)

**Section 3 — Verse-by-Verse Analysis:**

**Verse 1: قُلْ هُوَ ٱللَّهُ أَحَدٌ** [Al-Ikhlas 112:1]
- قُلْ: imperative of root ق-و-ل (hollow verb, irregular), command to Prophet
- هُوَ: ḍamīr al-sha'n (pronoun of affair) or mubtada — scholarly debate noted as Advanced callout
- ٱللَّهُ: khabar or second mubtada, nominative
- أَحَدٌ: khabar or second khabar, nominative, indefinite (why indefinite — emphasis on concept vs identity)
- Balagha: Why أَحَدٌ not وَاحِدٌ? (أَحَد = absolute uniqueness, وَاحِد = numerical one)

**Verse 2: ٱللَّهُ ٱلصَّمَدُ** [Al-Ikhlas 112:2]
- ٱللَّهُ: mubtada, nominative
- ٱلصَّمَدُ: khabar, nominative, definite — Root ص-م-د, pattern فَعَل
- Both definite = equation sentence ("Allah IS the Eternal Refuge")
- Balagha: ال on both = identification/equation, not description

**Verse 3: لَمْ يَلِدْ وَلَمْ يُولَدْ** [Al-Ikhlas 112:3]
- لَمْ: negation particle + jussive mood
- يَلِدْ: jussive (jazm, sukun), present form with past meaning, active voice
- يُولَدْ: jussive, passive voice (Form I passive)
- وَ: conjunction joining two parallel negations
- Balagha: Active then passive = negation from BOTH directions (not father, not son). Past-meaning present tense = ETERNAL negation, not just historical.

**Verse 4: وَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌ** [Al-Ikhlas 112:4]
- يَكُن: hollow verb كَانَ in jussive (shortened from يَكُون), ناقصة (incomplete verb)
- لَهُ: jarr wa-majrur, khabar of kaana (fronted)
- كُفُوًا: hal or tamyiz or second khabar, accusative, root ك-ف-ء "equal"
- أَحَدٌ: ism of kaana, nominative (delayed subject)
- Balagha: TAQDIM — normal order أَحَدٌ كُفُوًا لَهُ → inverted to لَهُ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌ for emphasis on "to HIM" (exclusivity) and delayed أَحَدٌ for finality. Word أَحَدٌ echoes V1 — ring composition.

**Section 4 — Surah-Level Synthesis:**
- Ring structure: أَحَدٌ (V1) echoed by أَحَدٌ (V4)
- Affirmation-Negation pattern: V1-2 affirm identity, V3-4 negate alternatives
- Grammar serving tawhid: every case, mood, and word order choice reinforces uniqueness
- Compare with Al-Fatiha analysis: different structure but same synthesis approach

**Section 5 — Practice:**
- 4 ExerciseBox:
  1. Verb analysis (guided): "Analyze the two jussive verbs in V3: يَلِدْ and يُولَدْ. What are their roots, forms, voices? Why does the voice change?"
  2. Taqdim analysis (intermediate): "In V4, the normal word order is inverted. Reconstruct the standard order, then explain the rhetorical effect of the inversion."
  3. Definiteness patterns (intermediate): "Track definiteness across all 4 verses. Why is أَحَدٌ indefinite in V1 but ٱلصَّمَدُ definite in V2?"
  4. Complete analysis (advanced): "Perform full 5-step analysis of Verse 2. Include all three levels: nahw, sarf, and balagha."

**Section 6 — Related Lessons:** L5.01 (method), L5.02 (Al-Fatiha), L4.10 (negation), L2.10-11 (kaana sisters), L5.05 (next)

**Imports:** ArabicExample, GrammarTable, Callout, ExerciseBox

**Section 1 — Introduction:**
- Context: "Al-Falaq (113) and An-Nas (114) are the final two surahs, traditionally recited together for protection. They share a parallel structure (قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ...) but differ in grammatical complexity. Analyzing them TOGETHER reveals how grammar adapts to convey different types of threats."
- Learning Objectives:
  1. Apply 5-step method to both surahs (11 total verses)
  2. Compare parallel grammatical structures across two surahs
  3. Identify how grammar adapts to content (external vs internal threats)
- Connection: Reference L5.01 method, L5.04 (short surah analysis). Note: "You've analyzed Al-Ikhlas (4 verses). Now analyze TWO related surahs to see how parallel structures VARY."

**Section 2 — Surah Al-Falaq Analysis (5 verses):**

**Verse 1: قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ ٱلْفَلَقِ** [Al-Falaq 113:1]
- قُلْ: imperative (same as Al-Ikhlas V1)
- أَعُوذُ: Form I present tense, first person, indicative (marfu'), Root ع-و-ذ
- بِرَبِّ: jarr wa-majrur, mudaf, Root ر-ب-ب
- ٱلْفَلَقِ: mudaf ilayh, genitive, Root ف-ل-ق "daybreak/splitting"
- Structure: verbal sentence within imperative frame

**Verses 2-5: مِن شَرِّ مَا خَلَقَ + three مِن شَرِّ clauses:**
- Repeated مِن شَرِّ structure: prepositional phrase + idafah
- مَا خَلَقَ (V2): relative clause with مَا
- غَاسِقٍ إِذَا وَقَبَ (V3): active participle + temporal clause
- ٱلنَّفَّاثَاتِ فِي ٱلْعُقَدِ (V4): feminine plural active participle + prepositional phrase
- حَاسِدٍ إِذَا حَسَدَ (V5): active participle + temporal clause
- Pattern: external threats (creation, darkness, magic, envy)

**Section 3 — Surah An-Nas Analysis (6 verses):**

**Verses 1-3: قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ ٱلنَّاسِ ﴿١﴾ مَلِكِ ٱلنَّاسِ ﴿٢﴾ إِلَٰهِ ٱلنَّاسِ ﴿٣﴾**
- Triple repetition of ٱلنَّاسِ — three attributes of Allah as badal or na't:
  رَبِّ (Lord/Nurturer), مَلِكِ (King/Sovereign), إِلَٰهِ (God/Deity)
- All genitive — in idafah with ٱلنَّاسِ
- Progression: nurture → governance → divinity (ascending attributes)
- Balagha: repetition of ٱلنَّاسِ for emphasis and rhythmic intensity

**Verses 4-6: مِن شَرِّ ٱلْوَسْوَاسِ ٱلْخَنَّاسِ ﴿٤﴾ ٱلَّذِي يُوَسْوِسُ فِي صُدُورِ ٱلنَّاسِ ﴿٥﴾ مِنَ ٱلْجِنَّةِ وَٱلنَّاسِ ﴿٦﴾**
- ٱلْوَسْوَاسِ ٱلْخَنَّاسِ: two intensive pattern nouns (فَعْلَال / فَعَّال), both genitive
- ٱلَّذِي: relative pronoun introducing relative clause
- يُوَسْوِسُ: Form I (or Form II — pattern analysis), present tense
- مِنَ ٱلْجِنَّةِ وَٱلنَّاسِ: source specification — jinn and humans
- Pattern: ONE internal threat (whisperer) vs Al-Falaq's FOUR external threats

**Section 4 — Comparative Synthesis:**
GrammarTable comparing the two surahs:
| Feature | Al-Falaq | An-Nas |
| Opening | قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ | قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ |
| Allah's attributes | 1 (رَبِّ ٱلْفَلَقِ) | 3 (رَبِّ, مَلِكِ, إِلَٰهِ) |
| Threats | 4 external (creation, darkness, magic, envy) | 1 internal (whispering) |
| Grammar emphasis | Active participles (غَاسِقٍ, حَاسِدٍ) | Intensive nouns (وَسْوَاس, خَنَّاس) |
| Threat source | External world | Internal heart |

Theological through grammar: MORE divine attributes (3) needed for internal threat → internal evil requires stronger refuge. Al-Falaq's threats are temporal (إِذَا clauses — "when"), An-Nas's threat is constant (present tense يُوَسْوِسُ).

**Section 5 — Practice:**
- 4 ExerciseBox:
  1. Parallel structure (guided): "Both surahs begin with قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ. How does the grammar AFTER this shared opening differ? Compare the next phrases."
  2. Participle analysis (intermediate): "Al-Falaq uses active participles (غَاسِقٍ, حَاسِدٍ). An-Nas uses intensive patterns (وَسْوَاس, خَنَّاس). Identify the root and pattern for each. Why are different patterns used?"
  3. Independent analysis (intermediate): "Perform complete i'rab for مِن شَرِّ مَا خَلَقَ [Al-Falaq 113:2]. Analyze each word: function, case, root."
  4. Comparative synthesis (advanced): "Why does An-Nas repeat ٱلنَّاسِ five times? What grammatical role does each occurrence play? What is the rhetorical effect of the repetition?"

**Section 6 — Related Lessons:** L5.01 (method), L5.04 (Al-Ikhlas), L3.16 (participles), L4.10 (negation), L5.06 (Juz Amma patterns)
