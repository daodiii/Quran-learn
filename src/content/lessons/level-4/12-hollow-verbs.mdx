## Introduction

You've learned the four-category weak verb classification system. Now you're ready to master the MOST COMMON weak verb category: hollow verbs (al-ajwaf). These are verbs with a weak letter (و or ي) in the MIDDLE position that contracts or drops during conjugation.

<ArabicExample

  transliteration="qul huwa llāhu aḥadun"
  translation="Say: He is Allah, the One"
  reference="Al-Ikhlas 112:1"
  highlight="قُلْ"
/>

The word **قُلْ** (qul) "say!" is the imperative form of the hollow verb قَالَْ (qāla) from root ق-و-ل (q-w-l). Notice that the middle و has completely DISAPPEARED in this form. This is the hollow verb pattern: the middle weak letter contracts into a long vowel (قَالَْ), becomes a different long vowel (يَقُولُ), or drops entirely (قُلْ) depending on the conjugation context.

## Understanding Hollow Verbs

**Plain English first:** Imagine a sandwich with soft cheese in the middle. When you squeeze it (add conjugation pressure), the cheese oozes out and changes shape — sometimes it spreads thin (long vowel), sometimes it disappears into the bread, sometimes it turns into something else. Hollow verbs work the same way: the soft middle letter responds to conjugation pressure by contracting, lengthening, or dropping.

### What Makes a Verb "Hollow"?

**[Hollow verb](/resources/glossary#fil-ajwaf)** (fiʿl ajwaf / فِعْلٌ أَجْوَفٌ) — verb with weak letter (و or ي) in the MIDDLE position (2nd radical)

**Examples:**
- **ق-و-ل** (q-w-l) "saying" → middle و
- **ك-و-ن** (k-w-n) "being" → middle و
- **ب-ي-ع** (b-y-ʿ) "selling" → middle ي
- **س-ي-ر** (s-y-r) "walking/traveling" → middle ي

**Why "hollow"?** The Arabic term أَجْوَفٌ (ajwaf) literally means "hollow" or "having a cavity." The middle of the verb is "hollowed out" — the weak letter contracts or disappears, leaving a gap that gets filled with long vowels or nothing.

### Sound vs Hollow: The Core Difference

Let's compare a sound verb with a hollow verb to see the pattern:

<GrammarTable>
| Form | Sound: ك-ت-ب (writing) | Hollow: ق-و-ل (saying) | Difference |
|------|----------------------|----------------------|------------|
| **Root** | ك-ت-ب (k-t-b) | ق-و-ل (q-w-l) | Middle letter is و (weak) |
| **Past 3MS** | كَتَبَ (kataba) | قَالَْ (qāla) | و → ا (contracted to long a) |
| **Present 3MS** | يَكْتُبُ (yaktubu) | يَقُولُ (yaqūlu) | و → و (became long u) |
| **Imperative 2MS** | ٱُكْتُبْ (uktub) | قُلْ (qul) | و → dropped! |
| **Pattern** | Predictable vowels | Middle letter changes | Weak letter responds to context |
</GrammarTable>

**Key observation:** The sound verb ك-ت-ب keeps all three root letters visible in every form. The hollow verb ق-و-ل's middle و transforms:
- In past tense: و becomes long ا (qāla)
- In present tense: و becomes long و (yaqūlu)
- In imperative: و drops completely (qul)

This isn't random — there are systematic rules!

## The Three Contraction Principles

**PRINCIPLE #1: Past Tense Contraction**

When conjugating hollow verbs in past tense, the middle weak letter (و or ي) contracts into a long vowel (ا).

**Rule:** Middle و or ي → long ا in past tense

Examples:
- ق-و-ل → قَالَْ (qāla) "he said"
- ك-و-ن → كَانَْ (kāna) "he was"
- ب-ي-ع → بَاعَْ (bāʿa) "he sold"
- س-ي-ر → سَارَْ (sāra) "he walked"

**Why?** In Arabic phonology, when a weak letter falls between two fatha vowels in Form I past tense (فَعَلَ pattern), it merges with them to form a long ā sound (fatha + و/ي + fatha → ā).

<Callout type="info" title="Compare with Sound Verb">

Sound verb: كَتَبَ (ka-ta-ba) — three distinct syllables, middle ت gets sukūn
Hollow verb: قَالَْ (qā-la) — two syllables, middle و merged into long ā

The hollow verb is effectively "compressed" from three syllables to two.

</Callout>

**PRINCIPLE #2: Present Tense Vowel Preservation**

In present tense, the middle weak letter becomes a long vowel matching its original quality:
- Middle و → long و (ū sound)
- Middle ي → long ي (ī sound)

Examples:
- ق-و-ل → يَقُولُ (yaqūlu) "he says" — و preserved as long ū
- ك-و-ن → يَكُونُ (yakūnu) "he is" — و preserved as long ū
- ب-ي-ع → يَبِيعُ (yabīʿu) "he sells" — ي preserved as long ī
- س-ي-ر → يَسِيرُ (yasīru) "he walks" — ي preserved as long ī

**Why?** The present tense pattern يَفْعُلُ (yafʿulu) puts damma or kasra on the middle letter position. The weak letter absorbs that vowel and becomes a long version of itself.

**PRINCIPLE #3: Imperative/Jussive Dropping**

In imperative and jussive forms, the middle weak letter DROPS completely when it would carry sukūn.

Examples:
- ق-و-ل → قُلْ (qul) "say!" — و dropped
- ك-و-ن → كُنْ (kun) "be!" — و dropped
- ب-ي-ع → بِعْ (biʿ) "sell!" — ي dropped
- س-ي-ر → سِرْ (sir) "walk!" — ي dropped

**Why?** In imperative/jussive, the middle position would get sukūn (قُوْلْ). But Arabic phonology doesn't allow و or ي with sukūn in this position — so it drops: قُ_لْ → قُلْ.

<Callout type="rule" title="Memorize These Three Principles, Not Individual Conjugations">

1. **Past tense:** Middle weak → long ا (qāla, kāna, bāʿa, sāra)
2. **Present tense:** Middle weak → long version of itself (yaqūlu, yabīʿu)
3. **Imperative/jussive:** Middle weak → drops (qul, kun, biʿ, sir)

Apply these three rules to ANY hollow verb, and you can conjugate it correctly!

</Callout>

## Hollow-Waw vs Hollow-Ya Subtypes

Hollow verbs divide into two subtypes based on which weak letter is in the middle:

### Hollow-Waw (Middle و)

**Most common hollow subtype** — middle radical is و

Common roots:
- **ق-و-ل** (q-w-l) "saying"
- **ك-و-ن** (k-w-n) "being"
- **ج-و-ء** (j-w-ʾ) "coming" (note: final hamza makes it also hamzated)
- **ن-و-ر** (n-w-r) "lighting/illuminating"
- **ص-و-م** (ṣ-w-m) "fasting"
- **ق-و-م** (q-w-m) "standing/rising"
- **ع-و-د** (ʿ-w-d) "returning"

**Pattern:**
- Past: Middle و → ا (قَالَْ, كَانَْ, جَاءَْ)
- Present: Middle و → ū (يَقُولُ, يَكُونُ, يَجِيءُ)
- Imperative: Middle و drops (قُلْ, كُنْ, جِئْ)

### Hollow-Ya (Middle ي)

**Less common than hollow-waw** — middle radical is ي

Common roots:
- **ب-ي-ع** (b-y-ʿ) "selling"
- **س-ي-ر** (s-y-r) "walking/traveling"
- **ط-ي-ر** (ṭ-y-r) "flying"
- **ن-ي-ل** (n-y-l) "attaining/reaching"
- **ز-ي-د** (z-y-d) "increasing"

**Pattern:**
- Past: Middle ي → ا (بَاعَْ, سَارَْ, طَارَْ)
- Present: Middle ي → ī (يَبِيعُ, يَسِيرُ, يَطِيرُ)
- Imperative: Middle ي drops (بِعْ, سِرْ, طِرْ)

**Key difference:** In present tense, hollow-waw verbs have damma on the prefix (يَقُولُ with ū), while hollow-ya verbs have kasra (يَبِيعُ with ī). This matches the original weak letter's vowel quality.

This demonstrates Principle #3: when the middle position would carry sukūn (due to suffix starting with consonant), the weak letter drops.

## Present Tense Hollow Verb Conjugation

Let's see the present tense pattern:

<VerbConjugation
  title="Present Tense: يَقُولُ (hollow-waw)"
  root="ق-و-ل"
>

| Person | Arabic | Transliteration | Pattern Note |
|--------|--------|----------------|--------------|
| 3MS (he) | يَقُولُ | yaqūlu | Middle و → long ū |
| 3FS (she) | تَقُولُ | taqūlu | Same pattern |
| 3MD (they two) | يَقُوُلَانِ | yaqūlāni | و preserved |
| 3FD (they two) | تَقُوُلَانِ | taqūlāni | و preserved |
| 3MP (they) | يَقُوُلُونَ | yaqūlūna | و preserved |
| 3FP (they) | يَقُلْنَ | yaqulna | و drops (before sukūn) |
| 2MS (you) | تَقُولُ | taqūlu | و preserved |
| 2FS (you) | تَقُوُلِينَ | taqūlīna | و preserved |
| 2MD (you two) | تَقُوُلَانِ | taqūlāni | و preserved |
| 2FD (you two) | تَقُوُلَانِ | taqūlāni | و preserved |
| 2MP (you) | تَقُوُلُونَ | taqūlūna | و preserved |
| 2FP (you) | تَقُلْنَ | taqulna | و drops (before sukūn) |
| 1S (I) | أَقُولُ | aqūlu | و preserved |
| 1P (we) | نَقُولُ | naqūlu | و preserved |

</VerbConjugation>

**Pattern note:** In present tense indicative mood (marfūʿ), the middle و becomes long ū in almost all forms. The ONLY exception is feminine plural (يَقُلْنَ, تَقُلْنَ) where the suffix ـْنَ starts with sukūn, causing the weak letter to drop.

**For hollow-ya verbs** like يَبِيعُ (yabīʿu) "he sells," the pattern is identical except middle ي → long ī instead of ū.

## Examples from Al-Ikhlas

Let's analyze hollow verbs from Surah Al-Ikhlas (Chapter 112), designated for this lesson:

### Example 1: قُلْ (qul) — Imperative

<ArabicExample
  arabic="قُلْ هُوَ ٱللَّهُ أَحَدٌ"
  transliteration="qul huwa llāhu aḥadun"
  translation="Say: He is Allah, the One"
  reference="Al-Ikhlas 112:1"
  highlight="قُلْ"
/>

**Morphological analysis:**

- **قُلْ** (qul)
  - Root: ق-و-ل (q-w-l) "saying"
  - Verb form: Form I hollow-waw
  - Tense/mood: Imperative, 2nd person masculine singular
  - Pattern: Weak letter و dropped (قُوْلْ → قُلْ)
  - Translation: "Say!" — commanding the Prophet (and by extension, believers)

**Contraction analysis:** The imperative pattern for sound verbs is ٱُفْعُلْ (ufʿul). For hollow verb ق-و-ل, it would be *ٱُقْوُلْ* (uqwul) — but the middle و with sukūn drops, leaving قُلْ (qul).

This is the opening command of Al-Ikhlas, introducing the declaration of Allah's oneness.

### Example 2: لَمْ يَلِدْ (lam yalid) — Jussive Hollow Verb

<ArabicExample
  arabic="لَمْ يَلِدْ وَلَمْ يُولَدْ"
  transliteration="lam yalid wa-lam yūlad"
  translation="He neither begets nor is born"
  reference="Al-Ikhlas 112:3"
  highlight="يَلِدْ"
/>

**Morphological analysis:**

- **يَلِدْ** (yalid)
  - Root: و-ل-د (w-l-d) "giving birth/begetting"
  - Verb form: Form I assimilated-hollow hybrid (initial و + hollow conjugation pattern)
  - Tense/mood: Present tense jussive (after لَمْ)
  - Pattern: Middle ل gets kasra, final د gets sukūn (jussive shortening)
  - Translation: "he begets" (negated by لَمْ = "he does not beget")

**Note:** This verb is technically ASSIMILATED (initial و), but it conjugates with some hollow-like features. The root و-ل-د loses its initial و in present tense (يَلِدُ not *يَوْلِدُ), demonstrating assimilated verb behavior. We'll cover this hybrid pattern in [L4.14 Assimilated Verbs](/learn/level-4/14-assimilated-verbs).

### Example 3: يَكُنْْ (yakun) — Jussive of كَانَْ

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَلَمْ يَكُنْ لَّهُۥ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌۢ"
  transliteration="wa-lam yakun lahū kufuwan aḥadun"
  translation="And there is none comparable to Him"
  reference="Al-Ikhlas 112:4"
  highlight="يَكُنْْ"
/>

**Morphological analysis:**

- **يَكُنْْ** (yakun)
  - Root: ك-و-ن (k-w-n) "being"
  - Verb form: Form I hollow-waw
  - Tense/mood: Present tense jussive (after لَمْ)
  - Pattern: Middle و dropped, final ن with sukūn (يَكُوْنْ → يَكُنْْ)
  - Translation: "he is" (negated by لَمْ = "he is not" / "there is not")

**Contraction analysis:** The indicative present is يَكُونُ (yakūnu) with long ū. In jussive mood (after لَمْ), the final damma drops, causing the pattern to become يَكُوْنْ. But Arabic doesn't allow و with sukūn in this position, so the و drops: يَكُنْْ (yakun).

This demonstrates Principle #3 perfectly: jussive mood triggers weak letter dropping.

<Callout type="info" title="كَانَْ — The Most Important Hollow Verb">

The verb كَانَْ (kāna) "to be/was" is one of the [kaana sisters](/resources/glossary#kana-sisters) you learned in [L2.11 Kaana and Sisters](/learn/level-2/11-kaana-sisters). It's a hollow verb (middle و) AND a defective verb (modifies nominal sentences).

Its conjugation follows ALL the hollow verb rules:
- Past: كَانَْ (kāna) — contracted middle
- Present: يَكُونُ (yakūnu) — long ū
- Jussive: يَكُنْْ (yakun) — middle dropped
- Imperative: كُنْ (kun) — middle dropped

Mastering كَانَْ means mastering hollow verbs!

</Callout>

## Summary

You've now mastered hollow verbs — the most common weak verb category:

**Key concepts:**
1. **Hollow verbs:** Middle weak letter (و or ي) that contracts or drops
2. **Three principles:** Past ا contraction, present long vowel preservation, imperative/jussive dropping
3. **Two subtypes:** Hollow-waw (middle و → ū) and hollow-ya (middle ي → ī)
4. **Systematic approach:** Apply principles, don't memorize individual conjugations

**Common hollow verbs to remember:**
- قَالَْ (qāla) / يَقُولُ (yaqūlu) / قُلْ (qul) — say/said
- كَانَْ (kāna) / يَكُونُ (yakūnu) / كُنْ (kun) — be/was
- جَاءَْ (jāʾa) / يَجِيءُ (yajīʾu) / جِئْ (jiʾ) — come/came
- صَامَْ (ṣāma) / يَصُومُ (yaṣūmu) / صُمْ (ṣum) — fast
- قَامَْ (qāma) / يَقُومُ (yaqūmu) / قُمْ (qum) — stand/rise
