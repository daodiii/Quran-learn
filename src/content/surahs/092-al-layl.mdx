# Al-Layl (The Night)

## Overview

- **Revelation:** Meccan
- **Verses:** 21
- **Theme:** This surah begins with oaths by the night, day, and the creation of male and female. It teaches that human efforts and paths differ vastly: those who give, are pious, and believe in goodness will be eased toward righteousness, while the miserly who deny goodness will be eased toward hardship. It promises ultimate reward and punishment, and emphasizes God's warning and guidance.
- **Grammar Focus:** Oath constructions with وَ (wa), conditional particles أَمَّا (amma) with فَ (fa-), Form II and Form IV verbs, comparative structures, future tense verbs, negation particles

<Callout type="tip" title="A Masterclass in Parallel Structure and Verb Form Diversity">
Al-Layl is one of the most beautifully structured surahs in the Quran, built on a perfect parallel contrast between two types of people. Verses 5-7 (the giver) mirror verses 8-10 (the miser) in exact grammatical structure: أَمَّا مَنْ + three verbs + فَسَنُيَسِّرُهُ لِ + superlative noun. The surah also showcases remarkable verb form diversity across its 21 verses: Form I (basic: يَغْشَىٰ, بَخِلَ, يَصْلَاهَا), Form II (intensive/causative: صَدَّقَ, كَذَّبَ, نُيَسِّرُ, يُجَنَّبُ), Form IV (transitive: أَعْطَىٰ, يُغْنِي, أَنذَرْتُكُمْ, يُؤْتِي), Form V (reflexive: تَجَلَّىٰ, تَرَدَّىٰ, تَوَلَّىٰ, تَلَظَّىٰ, يَتَزَكَّىٰ), Form VIII (reflexive: اتَّقَىٰ, ابْتِغَاءَ), and Form X (estimative: اسْتَغْنَىٰ). This makes it an extraordinary showcase for Arabic morphology, with Form V alone appearing five times with five different roots.
</Callout>

<Callout type="rule" title="The Oath Construction with وَ">
- Opening oath by the night -- وَ is oath particle taking genitive
- إِذَا introduces temporal clause: "when it covers [the day/earth]"
- يَغْشَىٰ present tense for recurring action, alif maqsura ending
- Object of يَغْشَىٰ is understood (the earth/day)
</Callout>

#### Nahw-Sarf-Balagha Synthesis

**Nahw (Syntax):** The verse opens with an oath construction (jumlat al-qasam). وَ is the oath particle (harf al-qasam) that governs the noun اللَّيْلِ into the genitive case. إِذَا introduces a temporal adverbial clause (zharfiyya) modifying the oath. The verb يَغْشَىٰ has a hidden subject (هُوَ referring to اللَّيْلِ) and a deleted object (understood as the earth or the day).

**Sarf (Morphology):** يَغْشَىٰ is a Form I present tense (mudari') verb from root غ-ش-ي on the pattern يَفْعَل. It ends with alif maqsura, indicating it is a defective verb (mu'tall al-lam). The present tense indicates a recurring, habitual action -- the night covers repeatedly by nature.

**Balagha (Rhetoric):** The surah opens with cosmic phenomena as witnesses: the night that covers and conceals. The choice of يَغْشَىٰ (covers/envelops) rather than simply "comes" evokes the totality of darkness spreading over the earth. This sets up the thematic contrast -- just as night covers, so too do people's deeds determine whether they are in spiritual darkness or light. The present tense suggests timeless, ongoing reality rather than a single event.

<Callout type="rule" title="Form V Reflexive: تَجَلَّىٰ">
- Continuation of oath series
- تَجَلَّىٰ Form V (reflexive): "to reveal itself, become manifest"
- Contrasts with verse 1: night covers, day reveals
- Perfect tense (past) used with إِذَا for completed action within the time frame

#### Nahw-Sarf-Balagha Synthesis

**Nahw (Syntax):** Parallel oath construction to verse 1, with وَ governing النَّهَارِ into the genitive. إِذَا again introduces a temporal clause. The shift from present tense (يَغْشَىٰ in v1) to past tense (تَجَلَّىٰ) is significant -- إِذَا can take either, but the past tense here conveys certainty and completion of the day's manifestation.

**Sarf (Morphology):** تَجَلَّىٰ is Form V (تَفَعَّلَ) from root ج-ل-و. Form V adds the reflexive/middle voice to the intensive Form II (جَلَّى "to reveal/unveil"), yielding "to reveal itself, become manifest." The alif maqsura ending marks it as a defective verb (mu'tall al-lam). The تَ prefix is the hallmark of Form V.

**Balagha (Rhetoric):** The contrast between يَغْشَىٰ (covers -- active concealment) and تَجَلَّىٰ (reveals itself -- reflexive manifestation) is the first of many antithetical pairs in this surah. Night actively covers, but day reveals itself, as if truth and goodness are self-manifesting while darkness requires effort. This duality foreshadows the surah's central contrast: those who give openly versus those who withhold.

<Callout type="rule" title="مَا as Ism Mawsul (Relative Pronoun)">
- مَا as ism mawsul: "He who" (referring to Allah the Creator)

- Third and final oath before the answer (jawab al-qasam)

#### Nahw-Sarf-Balagha Synthesis

**Nahw (Syntax):** The third oath, but with a different structure: وَ + مَا (relative pronoun) rather than وَ + definite noun. مَا functions as ism mawsul (relative pronoun) meaning "He who," occupying the place of a genitive after the oath particle. The relative clause (silat al-mawsul) is خَلَقَ الذَّكَرَ وَالْأُنثَىٰ. الذَّكَرَ is the direct object (maf'ul bihi) in the accusative, and الْأُنثَىٰ is coordinated with it (ma'tuf 'alayhi).

**Sarf (Morphology):** خَلَقَ is Form I past tense from root خ-ل-ق, one of the most fundamental verbs in Quranic Arabic. الذَّكَرَ from root ذ-ك-ر on pattern فَعَل and الْأُنثَىٰ from root أ-ن-ث on pattern فُعْلَى represent the fundamental gender pair in Arabic grammar and creation.

**Balagha (Rhetoric):** The three oaths form a crescendo: night (covering), day (revealing), and the Creator of male and female (the ultimate duality). Each oath introduces a pair of opposites -- night/day, covering/revealing, male/female -- preparing the listener for the surah's central contrast between two types of human beings. Swearing by the Creator rather than the creation in the third oath elevates the discourse to the divine level, establishing God's authority to judge the diverse paths humans take.

<Callout type="rule" title="إِنَّ...لَ Double Emphasis: The Jawab al-Qasam">
- إِنَّ... لَ double emphasis structure
- This is the answer (jawab) to the oath series (verses 1-3)
- سَعْيَكُمْ: human efforts/actions/strivings
- شَتَّىٰ indeclinable adjective meaning "diverse, various, scattered"
- Sets up the contrast in following verses: different types of effort
</Callout>

#### Nahw-Sarf-Balagha Synthesis

**Nahw (Syntax):** This verse is the jawab al-qasam (answer to the oath) -- the main clause that the three oaths in verses 1-3 were building toward. إِنَّ governs سَعْيَكُمْ as its subject (ism inna) in the accusative. The لَ prefix on شَتَّىٰ is lam al-ibtida' (the lam of commencement), creating a double emphasis structure (إِنَّ...لَ) that is one of the strongest emphasis constructions in Arabic. شَتَّىٰ is the predicate (khabar inna) in the nominative, though it is indeclinable due to its alif maqsura ending.

**Sarf (Morphology):** سَعْيَ is a masdar (verbal noun) from root س-ع-ي on the فَعْل pattern, meaning "striving, effort, endeavor." The attached pronoun كُمْ (2nd person plural) makes it a possessive: "your striving." شَتَّىٰ from root ش-ت-ت on the فَعْلَى pattern is a plural adjective meaning "diverse, scattered, various" -- it indicates complete divergence.

**Balagha (Rhetoric):** After three cosmic oaths, the answer is startlingly concise: just four words. The double emphasis (إِنَّ...لَ) indicates this truth is being solemnly declared -- human efforts diverge fundamentally. The word شَتَّىٰ (scattered/diverse) from the root meaning "to scatter" suggests not merely difference but radical divergence -- paths so different they cannot be reconciled. This single verse is the thesis statement that the rest of the surah elaborates.

<Callout type="rule" title="The أَمَّا...فَ Construction and Verb Forms IV and VIII">
- فَأَمَّا...فَ construction: "as for X, then Y" (Y comes in verse 7)
- مَنْ relative pronoun: "whoever"
- أَعْطَىٰ Form IV: "to give [in charity]" -- the hamza prefix (أَ) makes root ع-ط-و transitive
- اتَّقَىٰ Form VIII: "to be pious, fear God, be mindful" -- from root و-ق-ي with assimilation of the و into the تَ
- Both verbs with alif maqsura ending
</Callout>

#### Nahw-Sarf-Balagha Synthesis

**Nahw (Syntax):** This verse begins the first branch of the أَمَّا...فَ conditional construction. فَ is fa' al-tafsil (the fa' of detailing) connecting to the thesis in verse 4. أَمَّا is a conditional particle that requires فَ in its apodosis (which appears in verse 7). مَنْ is an indefinite relative pronoun functioning as the mubtada' (subject). The two verbs أَعْطَىٰ and اتَّقَىٰ are the sila (relative clause) of مَنْ.

**Sarf (Morphology):** أَعْطَىٰ is Form IV (أَفْعَلَ) from root ع-ط-و. Form IV adds transitivity -- while عَطَا means "to extend," أَعْطَى means "to give (something to someone)." اتَّقَىٰ is Form VIII (افْتَعَلَ) from root و-ق-ي. The original form would be اوْتَقَىٰ, but the weak first radical و assimilates into the تَ, yielding اتَّقَىٰ. Form VIII adds reflexivity: "to protect oneself" = "to be God-conscious."

**Balagha (Rhetoric):** The three characteristics of the righteous person (giving, fearing God, believing in goodness) are presented in ascending order of spiritual depth: أَعْطَىٰ (outward action -- generosity), اتَّقَىٰ (internal state -- God-consciousness), and صَدَّقَ بِالْحُسْنَىٰ (belief -- affirming ultimate truth). The progression moves from hand to heart to soul. Each verb ends in alif maqsura, creating a powerful rhythmic unity across the three verses (5-6).

**Sarf (Morphology):** الْهُدَىٰ from root ه-د-ي on the فُعَل pattern is a masdar (verbal noun) meaning "guidance." It ends with alif maqsura, making it indeclinable for visible case marking. The definite article الـ indicates comprehensive, complete guidance -- not some guidance but THE guidance.

**Balagha (Rhetoric):** After describing the two divergent paths (ease and hardship), God declares His own role: providing guidance. The fronting of عَلَيْنَا (upon Us) is theologically significant -- God takes ownership of guidance before naming it. The preposition عَلَى with its sense of obligation (indicating duty that is "upon" someone) conveys that guidance is not merely offered but is a divine responsibility that God has imposed upon Himself. Humans choose their path, but God ensures the path of guidance is always available.

The superlative الْأَشْقَى (the MOST wretched) further narrows the category, suggesting that the fire is reserved for the extreme case. This pairs with الْأَتْقَى (the MOST righteous) in verse 17, creating another superlative contrast.

| 5 | عِندَهُ | 'indahu | - | Adverb of place + attached pronoun | Adverbial (zarf makan), هُ 3rd person pronoun in genitive | with him/in his presence |
| 6 | مِن | min | - | Particle - preposition | Not declinable (mabni), governs genitive | from/of |
| 7 | نِّعْمَةٍ | ni'matin | ن-ع-م | Noun - feminine, singular, indefinite | Genitive (majrur) after مِن, with tanwin kasratayn | a favor/blessing |
| 8 | تُجْزَىٰ | tujza | ج-ز-ي | Verb - Form I, present passive, 3rd person feminine singular | Passive verb in subjunctive (mansub), describes نِّعْمَةٍ | it is rewarded |
</GrammarTable>

<Callout type="rule" title="Negation of Ulterior Motive: Complex Syntax">
- مَا negates: he doesn't give to repay a favor
- لِأَحَدٍ عِندَهُ: "for anyone in his presence" = who has done him a favor
- مِن نِّعْمَةٍ: partitive مِن "any favor" -- emphasizes that there is no favor at all
- تُجْزَىٰ passive: "to be rewarded/repaid"
- Emphasizes sincerity: giving purely for God, not to repay debts or gain social advantage
</Callout>

#### Nahw-Sarf-Balagha Synthesis

**Nahw (Syntax):** This is one of the more complex sentences in the surah. مَا is a negation particle for nominal sentences. لِأَحَدٍ is a prepositional phrase, and عِندَهُ is an adverb of place modifying أَحَدٍ. مِن is za'ida (extra/emphatic), added after negation to emphasize the totality of the denial -- "not from ANY favor." نِّعْمَةٍ is the logical subject of the negated sentence (mubtada' in the analysis of some grammarians). تُجْزَىٰ is a passive verbal sentence functioning as a sifa (adjective) describing نِّعْمَةٍ.

**Sarf (Morphology):** نِّعْمَة from root ن-ع-م on the فِعْلَة pattern means "blessing, favor, grace." تُجْزَىٰ is the passive of Form I (يُفْعَلُ) from root ج-ز-ي meaning "to be repaid/rewarded." The passive indicates that the favor is not being repaid -- the giving has no transactional motive.

**Balagha (Rhetoric):** This verse clarifies the nature of the righteous person's giving: it is not an exchange. The emphatic negation (مَا...مِن) leaves no room for ulterior motive -- there is not even a TRACE of a favor being repaid. The elaborate syntax (prepositional phrases, adverb, partitive مِن, passive verb) creates a thorough, legally precise denial of any motive other than sincerity. In a surah built on contrasts, this verse establishes the purest possible motive for generosity.

Adding الْأَعْلَىٰ at the end elevates the entire construction, reminding the listener that the One whose pleasure is sought is above all things.

- وَلَسَوْفَ triple emphasis: وَ + لَ + سَوْفَ
- سَوْفَ stronger future particle than سَ (more distant/emphatic future)

#### Nahw-Sarf-Balagha Synthesis

**Nahw (Syntax):** This final verse is remarkably concise -- just four words. وَ is a conjunction, لَ is lam al-ibtida' (emphasis), and سَوْفَ is the distant/emphatic future particle. Together, وَلَسَوْفَ combines three elements for maximum emphasis on the certainty of the future event. يَرْضَىٰ is the main verb with a hidden subject (هُوَ, referring to الْأَتْقَى). No object is expressed -- his satisfaction will be absolute and all-encompassing.

**Sarf (Morphology):** يَرْضَىٰ is Form I (يَفْعَلُ) from root ر-ض-ي, a defective verb (mu'tall al-lam) meaning "to be pleased, satisfied." The alif maqsura ending is characteristic of this verb class. سَوْفَ (as opposed to سَ) indicates a more distant and emphatic future, suggesting the ultimate, everlasting nature of this satisfaction.

**Balagha (Rhetoric):** The surah's final verse is a masterpiece of concision. After 20 verses of elaborate parallelism, cosmic oaths, and detailed descriptions, the conclusion is four simple words: "And he will surely be satisfied." The contrast between the complexity of the journey and the simplicity of the reward is itself eloquent. The triple emphasis (وَ + لَ + سَوْفَ) leaves no doubt. The absence of an object for يَرْضَىٰ is deliberate -- his satisfaction will not be limited to any one thing but will be complete, total, and eternal. This open-endedness is the ultimate reward: a satisfaction beyond description.

<ExerciseBox question="Analyze the parallel structure of the أَمَّا...فَ construction in verses 5-7 and 8-10. For each pair, identify the antonymous verbs, their verb forms, and explain how the grammatical parallelism reinforces the thematic contrast.">

**Verse 5 vs. Verse 8 (First pair of verbs):**
- أَعْطَىٰ (Form IV, root ع-ط-و) = "he gave" -- transitive, outward action of generosity
- بَخِلَ (Form I, root ب-خ-ل) = "he withheld" -- basic state of miserliness
- Note: the giver's action requires a HIGHER verb form (IV) reflecting effort, while the miser's action is basic Form I -- withholding requires no effort

**Verse 5 vs. Verse 8 (Second pair of verbs):**
- اتَّقَىٰ (Form VIII, root و-ق-ي) = "he feared God" -- reflexive awareness of divine accountability
- اسْتَغْنَىٰ (Form X, root غ-ن-ي) = "he considered himself self-sufficient" -- estimative self-deception
- Form VIII (reflexive protection of oneself) contrasts with Form X (considering oneself possessing a quality)

**Verse 6 vs. Verse 9 (Third verb):**
- صَدَّقَ بِالْحُسْنَىٰ (Form II, root ص-د-ق) = "he believed in the best"
- كَذَّبَ بِالْحُسْنَىٰ (Form II, root ك-ذ-ب) = "he denied the best"
- Both Form II (intensive), both with بِالْحُسْنَىٰ -- ONLY the verb changes, making the contrast maximally sharp

**Verse 7 vs. Verse 10 (Result clause):**
- فَسَنُيَسِّرُهُ لِلْيُسْرَىٰ = "We will ease him toward ease"
- فَسَنُيَسِّرُهُ لِلْعُسْرَىٰ = "We will ease him toward hardship"
- IDENTICAL syntax; only الْيُسْرَىٰ/الْعُسْرَىٰ differs -- one word changes the eternal destination

**Structural observation:** The parallelism is so precise that changing just one word per verse transforms the entire meaning. This is the surah's rhetorical genius: identical grammar, opposite content, showing that the divergence in human paths (شَتَّىٰ, verse 4) comes from individual choices, not from divine caprice.
</ExerciseBox>

<ExerciseBox question="Identify every verb form (I through X) that appears in Surah Al-Layl. For each form, list the verb(s), their root(s), and explain what semantic meaning the form adds to the base root.">

**Form I (فَعَلَ) -- Basic meaning:**
- يَغْشَىٰ (غ-ش-ي) = "it covers" -- simple action
- خَلَقَ (خ-ل-ق) = "He created" -- simple action
- بَخِلَ (ب-خ-ل) = "he was miserly" -- state/quality (فَعِلَ pattern)
- يَصْلَاهَا (ص-ل-ي) = "he burns in it" -- simple action
- يَرْضَىٰ (ر-ض-ي) = "he is satisfied" -- state

**Form II (فَعَّلَ) -- Intensive/Causative:**
- صَدَّقَ (ص-د-ق) = "he believed firmly" (intensive of "being truthful")
- كَذَّبَ (ك-ذ-ب) = "he denied vehemently" (intensive of "lying")
- نُيَسِّرُهُ (ي-س-ر) = "We will make easy" (causative of "being easy")
- يُجَنَّبُهَا (ج-ن-ب) = "he is kept to the side" (causative passive, "to cause to be on the side")

**Form IV (أَفْعَلَ) -- Transitive/Causative:**
- أَعْطَىٰ (ع-ط-و) = "he gave" (making someone possess something)
- يُغْنِي (غ-ن-ي) = "it avails/suffices" (making someone self-sufficient)
- أَنذَرْتُكُمْ (ن-ذ-ر) = "I warned you" (causing awareness of danger)
- يُؤْتِي (أ-ت-ي) = "he gives" (causing something to come to someone)

**Form V (تَفَعَّلَ) -- Reflexive of Form II:**
- تَجَلَّىٰ (ج-ل-و) = "it revealed itself" (reflexive of unveiling)
- تَرَدَّىٰ (ر-د-ي) = "he fell/destroyed himself" (reflexive of causing to fall)
- تَلَظَّىٰ (ل-ظ-ي) = "it blazes fiercely" (intensive reflexive burning)
- تَوَلَّىٰ (و-ل-ي) = "he turned himself away" (reflexive of turning)
- يَتَزَكَّىٰ (ز-ك-و) = "he purifies himself" (reflexive of purification)

**Form VIII (افْتَعَلَ) -- Reflexive/Middle voice:**
- اتَّقَىٰ (و-ق-ي) = "he protected himself [from sin]" = "he feared God"
- ابْتِغَاءَ (ب-غ-ي) = "seeking [for oneself]" (verbal noun)

**Form X (اسْتَفْعَلَ) -- Seeking/Estimative:**
- اسْتَغْنَىٰ (غ-ن-ي) = "he considered himself self-sufficient" (estimative: deemed himself غَنِيّ)

**Summary:** Six of the ten Arabic verb forms appear across 21 verses. Form V is the most frequent (5 verbs), followed by Form IV (4 verbs) and Form II (4 verbs). The diversity of verb forms is one of the surah's most distinctive grammatical features.
</ExerciseBox>

<ExerciseBox question="Examine the oath construction in verses 1-4. Identify: (a) the three objects of the oath, (b) how each creates a duality/contrast, (c) the jawab al-qasam, and (d) why this particular oath sequence leads to this particular answer.">

**(a) The three objects of the oath:**
1. وَالَّيْلِ إِذَا يَغْشَىٰ -- "By the night when it covers"
2. وَالنَّهَارِ إِذَا تَجَلَّىٰ -- "By the day when it appears"
3. وَمَا خَلَقَ الذَّكَرَ وَالْأُنثَىٰ -- "By the One who created male and female"

Each is introduced by وَ (oath particle), putting the following noun in the genitive (majrur).

**(b) The dualities/contrasts in each oath:**
1. Night (covers/conceals) vs. Day (reveals/manifests) -- darkness vs. light
2. يَغْشَىٰ (present tense, ongoing) vs. تَجَلَّىٰ (past tense, completed) -- process vs. achievement
3. الذَّكَرَ (male) vs. الْأُنثَىٰ (female) -- fundamental biological duality

Each oath presents a PAIR of opposites that work together in God's creation.

**(c) The jawab al-qasam (answer to the oath):**
إِنَّ سَعْيَكُمْ لَشَتَّىٰ -- "Indeed, your efforts are diverse"

Grammatically marked by إِنَّ...لَ (double emphasis), which is the standard marker of jawab al-qasam after وَ oaths.

**(d) Why this oath sequence leads to this answer:**
The three oaths establish that DUALITY is built into creation itself: night/day, covering/revealing, male/female. These are not defects but divine design. The answer then extends this principle to human effort: just as creation has inherent dualities, human striving (سَعْي) is inherently diverse (شَتَّى). Some strive toward good (giving, fearing God, believing) and some toward evil (withholding, self-sufficiency, denial).

The logical progression: God created the world in contrasting pairs → therefore human effort also diverges into contrasting paths → and the rest of the surah (verses 5-21) details these two divergent paths and their consequences.
</ExerciseBox>

## Grammar Summary

<Callout type="note" title="Key Grammar Concepts in This Surah">
- **Oath constructions with وَ**: Verses 1-3 use وَ as oath particle taking genitive case
- **Temporal clauses with إِذَا**: Verses 1, 2, 11 use إِذَا to introduce when-clauses
- **إِنَّ emphasis structures**: Verses 4, 12, 13 use إِنَّ for emphasis, with لَ for double emphasis
- **أَمَّا...فَ conditional construction**: Verses 5-7 and 8-10 demonstrate "as for X, then Y" structure with perfect parallelism
- **Future tense particles**: سَ (verses 7, 10, 17) and سَوْفَ (verse 21) indicate future actions
- **Comparative/superlative structures**: الْيُسْرَىٰ vs. الْعُسْرَىٰ (ease vs. hardship), الْأَشْقَى vs. الْأَتْقَى (most wretched vs. most righteous), الْحُسْنَىٰ (the best), الْأَعْلَىٰ (the Most High)
- **Form II verbs (intensive/causative)**: صَدَّقَ, كَذَّبَ, نُيَسِّرُهُ, يُجَنَّبُهَا (passive)
- **Form IV verbs**: أَعْطَىٰ, يُغْنِي, أَنذَرْتُكُمْ, يُؤْتِي
- **Form V verbs (reflexive)**: تَجَلَّىٰ, تَرَدَّىٰ, تَوَلَّىٰ, تَلَظَّىٰ, يَتَزَكَّىٰ
- **Form VIII verbs**: اتَّقَىٰ, ابْتِغَاءَ (verbal noun)
- **Form X verb**: اسْتَغْنَىٰ (considering oneself to possess a quality)
- **Passive voice constructions**: يُجَنَّبُهَا, تُجْزَىٰ
