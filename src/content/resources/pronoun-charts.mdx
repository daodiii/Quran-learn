# Pronoun Reference Charts

Complete reference for all four categories of Arabic pronouns (الضَّمَائِرُ / ad-damaa'iru) used throughout the Quran. This reference uses fully vocalized Arabic text with Hamza above alif (ʾa / أَ) and Hamza below alif (ʾi / إِ) marks where appropriate.

## Quick Navigation

- [Attached Pronouns](#attached-pronouns) - Suffix pronouns on verbs, nouns, and particles
- [Demonstrative Pronouns](#demonstrative-pronouns) - Pointing words (this/that)
- [Relative Pronouns](#relative-pronouns) - Connecting words (who/which/that)

## Independent Pronouns

**الضَّمَائِرُ الْمُنْفَصِلَةُ** (ad-damaa'iru l-munfasilah) - Independent/Detached Pronouns

These pronouns stand alone as complete words, typically serving as subjects in nominal and verbal sentences.

    ["3rd Singular (m)", "هُوَ", "huwa", "he/it"],

<ArabicExample
  arabic="إِنَّكَ أَنْتَ الْعَلِيمُ الْحَكِيمُ"
  transliteration="innaka anta l-'aliimu l-hakiim"
  translation="Indeed, You are the All-Knowing, the All-Wise"
  reference="Surah Al-Baqarah 2:32"
  highlight="أَنْتَ"
/>

<Callout type="note" title="Usage in Quranic Grammar">
Independent pronouns often appear in nominal sentences (جُمْلَة اسْمِيَّة) as subjects, or for emphasis when added to verbal sentences. The pronoun **أَنْتَ** (you) in the example above emphasizes the subject.
</Callout>

## Attached Pronouns

**الضَّمَائِرُ الْمُتَّصِلَةُ** (ad-damaa'iru l-muttasilah) - Attached/Connected Pronouns

These pronouns appear as suffixes attached to verbs, nouns, or particles. Their meaning changes based on what they attach to.

### On Verbs (Object Pronouns)

When attached to verbs, these pronouns indicate the **object** of the action.

  rows={[

    ["2nd Singular (f)", "-ki", "رَآكِْ", "he saw you (f)"],
    ["2nd Dual (m+f)", "-kumaa", "رَآكُمَاْ", "he saw you two"],
    ["2nd Plural (m)", "-kum", "رَآكُمْ", "he saw you all (m)"],
    ["2nd Plural (f)", "-kunna", "رَآكُنَّ", "he saw you all (f)"],
    ["3rd Singular (m)", "-hu", "رَآهُْ", "he saw him/it"],
    ["3rd Singular (f)", "-haa", "رَآهَاْ", "he saw her/it"],
    ["3rd Dual (m+f)", "-humaa", "رَآهُمَاْْ", "he saw them two"],
    ["3rd Plural (m)", "-hum", "رَآهُمْ", "he saw them (m)"],
    ["3rd Plural (f)", "-hunna", "رَآهُنَّ", "he saw them (f)"],
  ]}
/>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="رَبَّنَا لَا تُؤَاخِذْنَا إِن نَّسِينَا أَوْ أَخْطَأْنَا"
  transliteration="rabbanaa laa tu'aakhidhnaa in nasiinaa aw akhta'naa"
  translation="Our Lord, do not impose blame upon us if we have forgotten or erred"
  reference="Surah Al-Baqarah 2:286"
  highlight="تُؤَاخِذْنَا"
/>

<Callout type="note" title="Vowel Harmony">
Notice that the pronoun suffix **ـنَا** (us) in **تُؤَاخِذْنَا** (impose blame upon us) attaches directly to the verb root. The vowel changes based on the verb form and context.
</Callout>

When attached to nouns, these pronouns indicate **possession** (my, your, his, her, etc.).

<ArabicExample
  arabic="الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ"
  transliteration="al-hamdu lillaahi rabbi l-'aalamiin"
  translation="All praise is due to Allah, Lord of the worlds"
  reference="Surah Al-Fatihah 1:2"
  highlight="رَبِّ"
/>

<Callout type="rule" title="Case Ending Changes">
When a pronoun attaches to a noun, the noun loses its tanween (nunation) and becomes definite. Notice **رَبِّ** (Lord of) with the genitive case ending attached to **الْعَالَمِْينَ** (al-'aalameen, the worlds).
</Callout>

When attached to prepositions and certain particles, pronouns indicate the object of the preposition.

    ["بِـ (with/by)", "بِيَ", "biya", "with me"],
    ["بِـ (with/by)", "بِكَ", "bika", "with you (m)"],
    ["بِـ (with/by)", "بِهِ", "bihi", "with him"],

<ArabicExample
  arabic="إِيَّاكَ نَعْبُدُ وَإِيَّاكَ نَسْتَعِينُ"
  transliteration="iyyaaka na'budu wa-iyyaaka nasta'een"
  translation="You alone we worship, and You alone we ask for help"
  reference="Surah Al-Fatihah 1:5"
  highlight="إِيَّاكَ"
/>

<Callout type="note" title="Emphasis with إِيَّا">
The particle **إِيَّا** (iyyaa) is a special emphasis particle that takes attached pronouns for emphasis. Here **إِيَّاكَ** means "You (and You alone)" - emphasizing exclusive worship and reliance on Allah.
</Callout>

## Demonstrative Pronouns

**أَسْمَاءُ الْإِشَارَةِ** (asmaa'u l-ishaarah) - Demonstrative Pronouns/Nouns of Indication

These pronouns point to near or far objects, people, or concepts (this, that, these, those).

    ["Dual (m+f)", "هَٰذَاْنِ / هَٰذَيْنِ", "haadhaani / hadhayni", "these two"],

    ["Feminine Singular", "تِلْكَ", "tilka", "that (f)"],
    ["Dual (m+f)", "ذَانِكَ / ذَيْنِكَ", "dhaanika / dhaynika", "those two"],

<ArabicExample
  arabic="ذَٰلِكَ الْكِتَابُ لَا رَيْبَ فِيهِ هُدًى لِّلْمُتَّقِينَ"
  transliteration="dhaalika l-kitaabu laa rayba feehi hudan lil-muttaqeen"
  translation="This is the Book about which there is no doubt, a guidance for those conscious of Allah"
  reference="Surah Al-Baqarah 2:2"
  highlight="ذَٰلِكَ"
/>

<Callout type="note" title="Near vs. Far in Context">
In the Quran, **ذَٰلِكَ** (that) often refers to the Quran itself or concepts being discussed, even though grammatically it's a "far" demonstrative. The choice between near/far can indicate conceptual distance rather than physical distance.
</Callout>

## Relative Pronouns

**الْأَسْمَاءُ الْمَوْصُولَةُ** (al-asmaa'u l-mawsoolah) - Relative Pronouns/Relative Nouns

These pronouns connect clauses and introduce relative clauses (who, which, that, what).

### Specific Relative Pronouns

These change based on gender and number.

<GrammarTable
  caption="Specific Relative Pronouns"
  headers={["Gender/Number", "Arabic", "Transliteration", "Meaning"]}
  rows={[

    ["Feminine Singular", "الَّتِي", "allatee", "who/which/that (f.sg)"],
    ["Masculine Dual", "اللَّذَانِْ / اللَّذَيْنِ", "alladhaani / alladhayni", "who/which (m.dual)"],
    ["Feminine Dual", "اللَّتَانِْ / اللَّتَيْنِ", "allataani / allataayni", "who/which (f.dual)"],
    ["Masculine Plural", "الَّذِينَ", "alladheena", "who/which/that (m.pl)"],
    ["Feminine Plural", "اللَّاتِيِْ / اللَّوَاتِيِْ", "allaatee / allawaatee", "who/which/that (f.pl)"],
  ]}
/>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ الَّذِي خَلَقَ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضَ"
  transliteration="al-hamdu lillaahi lladhee khalaqa s-samaawaati wa-l-arda"
  translation="All praise is due to Allah, who created the heavens and the earth"
  reference="Surah Al-An'am 6:1"
  highlight="الَّذِي"
/>

### Common (Indeclinable) Relative Pronouns

These do not change for gender or number.

<GrammarTable
  caption="Common Relative Pronouns"
  headers={["Arabic", "Transliteration", "Meaning", "Usage"]}
  rows={[
    ["مَنْ", "man", "who/whoever", "For rational beings (people)"],
    ["مَا", "maa", "what/whatever", "For non-rational beings or concepts"],
    ["ذَا (after مَنْ/مَا)", "dhaa", "who/what", "Combined: مَنْ ذَا = who? / مَا ذَا = what?"],
  ]}
/>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَمَا تَفْعَلُوا مِنْ خَيْرٍ يَعْلَمْهُ اللَّهُ"
  transliteration="wa-maa taf'aloo min khayrin ya'lamhu llaah"
  translation="And whatever you do of good - Allah knows it"
  reference="Surah Al-Baqarah 2:197"
  highlight="مَا"
/>

<Callout type="rule" title="Relative Clause Structure">
A relative pronoun introduces a **صِلَةٌ** (silatun) - the relative clause that follows it. The relative clause must be a complete sentence (verbal or nominal) that describes or identifies the antecedent. In the example above, **تَفْعَلُواْ مِنْ خَيْرٍ** (taf'aloo min khayr) is the relative clause following **مَا**.
</Callout>

<GrammarTable
  caption="Interrogative vs. Relative Usage"
  headers={["Word", "As Question", "As Relative"]}
  rows={[
    ["مَنْ", "Who? (question)", "who/whoever (relative)"],
    ["مَا", "What? (question)", "what/whatever (relative)"],
    ["مَاذَاْ", "What? (question)", "what (relative)"],
    ["أَيْنَ", "Where? (question)", "where (relative with أَيْنَمَا)"],
  ]}
/>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَالَّذِينَ آمَنُوا وَعَمِلُوا الصَّالِحَاتِ أُولَٰئِكَ أَصْحَابُ الْجَنَّةِ"
  transliteration="wa-lladheena aamanoo wa-'amiloo s-saalihaati ulaa'ika as-haabu l-jannah"
  translation="But those who believed and did righteous deeds - those are the companions of Paradise"
  reference="Surah Al-Baqarah 2:82"
  highlight="الَّذِينَ"
/>

<Callout type="tip" title="Most Common in Quran">
The plural masculine **الَّذِينَ** (those who) is the most frequently used relative pronoun in the Quran, often describing believers: **الَّذِينَ آمَنُوَاْ** (alladheena aamanoo, those who believe).
</Callout>

## Summary

**Four Pronoun Categories:**

1. **Independent Pronouns** (الضَّمَائِرُ الْمُنْفَصِلَةُ) - Stand-alone words (I, you, he, she, we, they)
2. **Attached Pronouns** (الضَّمَائِرُ الْمُتَّصِلَةُ) - Suffixes on verbs (object), nouns (possession), particles (prepositional objects)
3. **Demonstrative Pronouns** (أَسْمَاءُ الْإِشَارَةِ) - Pointing words (this, that, these, those)
4. **Relative Pronouns** (الْأَسْمَاءُ الْمَوْصُولَةُ) - Connecting words (who, which, that, what)

<Callout type="note" title="Pronoun Agreement">
All pronouns in Arabic must agree with their antecedents in **gender**, **number**, and (for attached pronouns) sometimes **case**. Mastering pronoun forms is essential for understanding the precise references in Quranic verses.
</Callout>
