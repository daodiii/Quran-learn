## Overview: The Three Cases

Arabic nouns change their endings based on grammatical function within a sentence. These changes are called **case endings** (حَرَكَاتُ الْإِعْرَابِ).

## Singular Nouns (الْمُفْرَدُ)

Most singular nouns follow the **triptote** pattern — they take all three cases with **tanwīn** (التَّنْوِينُ) when indefinite.

### Triptote Pattern

<GrammarTable
  caption="Singular Noun Case Endings (Triptote)"
  headers={["Case", "Indefinite", "Definite", "Example (indefinite)", "Example (definite)"]}
  rows={[
    ["Nominative", "ـٌ (-un)", "ـُ (-u)", "كِتَابٌ (kitābun) 'a book'", "الْكِتَابُ (al-kitābu) 'the book'"],
    ["Accusative", "ـًا (-an)", "ـَ (-a)", "كِتَابًا (kitāban) 'a book'", "الْكِتَابَ (al-kitāba) 'the book'"],
    ["Genitive", "ـٍ (-in)", "ـِ (-i)", "كِتَابٍ (kitābin) 'a book'", "الْكِتَابِ (al-kitābi) 'the book'"],
  ]}
  rtl={false}
/>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="إِنَّ اللَّهَ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ"
  transliteration="inna llāha ʿalā kulli shayʾin qadīrun"
  translation="Indeed, Allah is over all things competent."
  reference="Surah Al-Baqarah 2:20"
  highlight="شَيْءٍ,قَدِيرٌ"
/>

**Analysis:**
- **شَيْءٍ** (shayʾin): Genitive indefinite after preposition كُلِّ (every)
- **قَدِيرٌ** (qadīrun): Nominative indefinite — predicate describing Allah

<details>
<summary><strong>Diptote Nouns (المَمْنُوعُ مِنَ الصَّرْفِ)</strong> — Special Pattern</summary>

Some nouns are **diptote** (مَمْنُوعٌ مِنَ الصَّرْفِ) — they never take tanwīn when indefinite and use fatḥah instead of kasrah in the genitive.

**Common diptote patterns:**
- Proper names (أَحْمَدُ, مَرْيَمُ)
- Adjective patterns like أَفْعَلُ (أَكْبَرُ, أَحْسَنُ)
- Broken plurals on patterns مَفَاعِلُ or فَوَاعِلُ (مَسَاجِدُ, صَحَارَى)

<GrammarTable
  caption="Diptote Case Endings"
  headers={["Case", "Indefinite", "Definite"]}
  rows={[
    ["Nominative", "ـُ (-u)", "ـُ (-u)"],
    ["Accusative", "ـَ (-a)", "ـَ (-a)"],
    ["Genitive", "ـَ (-a)", "ـِ (-i)"],
  ]}
  rtl={false}
/>

**Key difference:** Indefinite genitive uses **fatḥah** (ـَ), not kasrah.

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَلِلَّهِ الْأَسْمَاءُ الْحُسْنَىٰ فَادْعُوهُ بِهَا"
  transliteration="wa li-llāhi l-asmāʾu l-ḥusnā fa-dʿūhu bihā"
  translation="And to Allah belong the best names, so invoke Him by them."
  reference="Surah Al-A'raf 7:180"
  highlight="الْحُسْنَىٰ"
/>

**الْحُسْنَىٰ** (al-ḥusnā): Feminine superlative أَفْعَلُ pattern, diptote. Here definite nominative.

</details>

## Dual Nouns (الْمُثَنَّى)

The dual (الْمُثَنَّى) indicates exactly two of something. It has only **two case forms**: one for nominative, one for accusative/genitive.

<GrammarTable
  caption="Dual Noun Case Endings"
  headers={["Case", "Ending", "Example"]}
  rows={[
    ["Nominative", "ـَانِ (-āni) indefinite<br/>ـَانِ (-āni) definite", "رَجُلَانِ (rajulāni) 'two men'<br/>الرَّجُلَانِ (ar-rajulāni) 'the two men'"],
    ["Accusative/Genitive", "ـَيْنِ (-ayni) indefinite<br/>ـَيْنِ (-ayni) definite", "رَجُلَيْنِ (rajulayni) 'two men'<br/>الرَّجُلَيْنِ (ar-rajulayni) 'the two men'"],
  ]}
  rtl={false}
/>

<Callout type="rule">
The dual suffix includes the letter **nūn** (نٌ), which **drops** when the noun is in **iḍāfah** (possessive construct).

Example:
- **كِتَابَانِ** (kitābāni) "two books" → **كِتَابَا الطَّالِبِ** (kitābā aṭ-ṭālibi) "the student's two books"
</Callout>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="فَإِن كَانَتَا اثْنَتَيْنِ فَلَهُمَا الثُّلُثَانِ"
  transliteration="fa-in kānatā ithnatayni fa-lahumā th-thuluthāni"
  translation="But if there are two [daughters], for them is two-thirds."
  reference="Surah An-Nisa 4:11"
  highlight="اثْنَتَيْنِ,الثُّلُثَانِ"
/>

**Analysis:**
- **اثْنَتَيْنِ** (ithnatayni): Dual accusative (predicate after كَانَتَا)
- **الثُّلُثَانِ** (ath-thuluthāni): Dual nominative (subject)

**Related lesson:** L2.08 (Dual Nouns)

## Sound Masculine Plural (جَمْعُ الْمُذَكَّرِ السَّالِمِ)

The **sound masculine plural** applies to:
- Male human nouns (مُسْلِمُونَ muslimūna "Muslims")
- Masculine adjectives (صَالِحُونَ ṣāliḥūna "righteous ones")

<GrammarTable
  caption="Sound Masculine Plural Endings"
  headers={["Case", "Ending", "Example"]}
  rows={[
    ["Nominative", "ـُونَ (-ūna) indefinite<br/>ـُونَ (-ūna) definite", "مُسْلِمُونَ (muslimūna)<br/>المُسْلِمُونَ (al-muslimūna)"],
    ["Accusative/Genitive", "ـِينَ (-īna) indefinite<br/>ـِينَ (-īna) definite", "مُسْلِمِينَ (muslimīna)<br/>المُسْلِمِينَ (al-muslimīna)"],
  ]}
  rtl={false}
/>

<Callout type="rule">
Like the dual, the **nūn** drops in **iḍāfah**:

- **مُسْلِمُونَ** (muslimūna) → **مُسْلِمُو المَدِينَةِ** (muslimū al-madīnati) "the Muslims of the city"
</Callout>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَالْمُؤْمِنُونَ وَالْمُؤْمِنَاتُ بَعْضُهُمْ أَوْلِيَاءُ بَعْضٍ"
  transliteration="wa-l-muʾminūna wa-l-muʾminātu baʿḍuhum awliyāʾu baʿḍin"
  translation="The believing men and believing women are allies of one another."
  reference="Surah At-Tawbah 9:71"
  highlight="الْمُؤْمِنُونَ"
/>

**الْمُؤْمِنُونَ** (al-muʾminūna): Definite nominative plural (subject)

<details>
<summary><strong>More Examples: Sound Masculine Plural in Context</strong></summary>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا اتَّقُوا اللَّهَ حَقَّ تُقَاتِهِ وَلَا تَمُوتُنَّ إِلَّا وَأَنتُم مُّسْلِمُونَ"
  transliteration="yā ayyuhā lladhīna āmanū ttaqū llāha ḥaqqa tuqātihi wa-lā tamūtunna illā wa-antum muslimūna"
  translation="O you who have believed, fear Allah as He should be feared and do not die except as Muslims [in submission to Him]."
  reference="Surah Ali 'Imran 3:102"
  highlight="مُّسْلِمُونَ"
/>

**مُّسْلِمُونَ** (muslimūna): Nominative predicate after كَانَ-type construction

<ArabicExample
  arabic="كُنتُمْ خَيْرَ أُمَّةٍ أُخْرِجَتْ لِلنَّاسِ"
  transliteration="kuntum khayra ummatin ukhrijat li-n-nāsi"
  translation="You are the best nation produced [as an example] for mankind."
  reference="Surah Ali 'Imran 3:110"
/>

**Analysis:** When masculine plural nouns are indefinite and in iḍāfah, the nūn drops:
- **خَيْرَ** (khayra): Accusative predicate
- Would be خَيْرُونَ (khayrūna) if not in iḍāfah

</details>

## Sound Feminine Plural (جَمْعُ الْمُؤَنَّثِ السَّالِمِ)

The **sound feminine plural** ending **ـَاتٌ** applies to:
- Female nouns (مُسْلِمَاتٌ muslimātun "Muslim women")
- Many non-human plurals
- Some abstract nouns

<GrammarTable
  caption="Sound Feminine Plural Endings"
  headers={["Case", "Indefinite", "Definite"]}
  rows={[
    ["Nominative", "ـَاتٌ (-ātun)", "ـَاتُ (-ātu)"],
    ["Accusative", "ـَاتٍ (-ātin)", "ـَاتِ (-āti)"],
    ["Genitive", "ـَاتٍ (-ātin)", "ـَاتِ (-āti)"],
  ]}
  rtl={false}
/>

<Callout type="note">
Unlike the masculine plural, the sound feminine plural has **three separate case forms**, but accusative and genitive share the same ending.
</Callout>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="إِنَّ الْمُسْلِمِينَ وَالْمُسْلِمَاتِ وَالْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَالْمُؤْمِنَاتِ"
  transliteration="inna l-muslimīna wa-l-muslimāti wa-l-muʾminīna wa-l-muʾmināti"
  translation="Indeed, the Muslim men and Muslim women, the believing men and believing women..."
  reference="Surah Al-Ahzab 33:35"
  highlight="الْمُسْلِمَاتِ,الْمُؤْمِنَاتِ"
/>

**الْمُسْلِمَاتِ** and **الْمُؤْمِنَاتِ**: Both genitive after the particle إِنَّ (which governs accusative on the first element, then subsequent coordinated nouns take genitive).

<details>
<summary><strong>More Examples: Sound Feminine Plural Patterns</strong></summary>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَالصَّالِحَاتِ مِن نِّسَائِكُمْ"
  transliteration="wa-ṣ-ṣāliḥāti min nisāʾikum"
  translation="And the righteous women among your women"
  reference="Surah An-Nur 24:32"
  highlight="الصَّالِحَاتِ"
/>

**الصَّالِحَاتِ** (aṣ-ṣāliḥāti): Genitive after conjunction وَ in genitive context

**Pattern recognition:**
- Nominative indefinite: صَالِحَاتٌ (ṣāliḥātun)
- Accusative indefinite: صَالِحَاتٍ (ṣāliḥātin)
- Genitive indefinite: صَالِحَاتٍ (ṣāliḥātin)

The feminine plural is especially common for:
- Abstract concepts: حَسَنَاتٌ (ḥasanātun) "good deeds", سَيِّئَاتٌ (sayyiʾātun) "bad deeds"
- Collections: آيَاتٌ (āyātun) "signs/verses", كَلِمَاتٌ (kalimātun) "words"

</details>

## Broken Plurals (جَمْعُ التَّكْسِيرِ)

**Broken plurals** (جَمْعُ التَّكْسِيرِ) change the internal structure of the word. They follow the **same case endings as singular nouns**.

<GrammarTable
  caption="Broken Plural Case Endings"
  headers={["Case", "Triptote Indefinite", "Triptote Definite", "Diptote Indefinite", "Diptote Definite"]}
  rows={[
    ["Nominative", "ـٌ (-un)", "ـُ (-u)", "ـُ (-u)", "ـُ (-u)"],
    ["Accusative", "ـًا (-an)", "ـَ (-a)", "ـَ (-a)", "ـَ (-a)"],
    ["Genitive", "ـٍ (-in)", "ـِ (-i)", "ـَ (-a)", "ـِ (-i)"],
  ]}
  rtl={false}
/>

**Common broken plural patterns:**
- فُعُولٌ (بُيُوتٌ buyūtun "houses" from بَيْتٌ)
- فِعَالٌ (رِجَالٌ rijālun "men" from رَجُلٌ)
- أَفْعَالٌ (أَوْلَادٌ awlādun "children" from وَلَدٌ)
- فُعَلَاءُ (عُلَمَاءُ ʿulamāʾu "scholars" from عَالِمٌ) — **diptote**

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَإِذْ قَالَ رَبُّكَ لِلْمَلَائِكَةِ إِنِّي جَاعِلٌ فِي الْأَرْضِ خَلِيفَةً"
  transliteration="wa-idh qāla rabbuka li-l-malāʾikati innī jāʿilun fī l-arḍi khalīfatan"
  translation="And [mention] when your Lord said to the angels, 'Indeed, I will make upon the earth a successive authority.'"
  reference="Surah Al-Baqarah 2:30"
  highlight="لِلْمَلَائِكَةِ"
/>

**لِلْمَلَائِكَةِ** (li-l-malāʾikati): Broken plural of مَلَكٌ (malakun "angel"), genitive after preposition لِـ

**Related lesson:** L2.12 (Broken Plurals)

## The Five Nouns (الأَسْمَاء الخَمْسَة)

Five special nouns use **long vowels** (وٌ، اٌ، يٌ) instead of short vowel case endings when they are:
1. Singular
2. Indefinite or in iḍāfah (possessive)
3. Not followed by the first-person possessive suffix (ـي)

The five nouns are:
- **أَبٌ** (abun) "father"
- **أَخٌ** (akhun) "brother"
- **حَمٌ** (ḥamun) "father-in-law"
- **فُو** (fū) "mouth"
- **ذُو** (dhū) "possessor of"

<GrammarTable
  caption="Five Nouns Case Endings"
  headers={["Case", "Ending Pattern", "Example: أَبٌ (father)"]}
  rows={[
    ["Nominative", "Long ū (ـُو)", "أَبُوهُ (abūhu) 'his father'"],
    ["Accusative", "Long ā (ـَا)", "أَبَاهُ (abāhu) 'his father'"],
    ["Genitive", "Long ī (ـِي)", "أَبِيهِ (abīhi) 'his father'"],
  ]}
  rtl={false}
/>

<Callout type="rule" title="When Five Nouns Use Regular Endings">
These nouns revert to **regular singular case endings** when:
- Definite with the article **ال** (rare usage)
- Followed by the suffix **ـِي** (my): أَبِي (abī) "my father" — same form in all cases
</Callout>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَاتَّبَعْتُ مِلَّةَ آبَائِي إِبْرَاهِيمَ وَإِسْحَاقَ وَيَعْقُوبَ"
  transliteration="wa-ttabaʿtu millata ābāʾī ibrāhīma wa-isḥāqa wa-yaʿqūba"
  translation="And I have followed the religion of my fathers, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob."
  reference="Surah Yusuf 12:38"
  highlight="آبَائِي"
/>

**آبَائِي** (ābāʾī): Plural of أَب (ab "father"), not the special five-noun pattern. Genitive after مِلَّةَ.

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَرَفَعَ أَبَوَيْهِ عَلَى الْعَرْشِ"
  transliteration="wa-rafaʿa abawayhi ʿalā l-ʿarshi"
  translation="And he raised his parents upon the throne."
  reference="Surah Yusuf 12:100"
  highlight="أَبَوَيْهِ"
/>

**أَبَوَيْهِ** (abawayhi): Dual of أَب, accusative (direct object). Shows **wāw** before **dual suffix**.

<details>
<summary><strong>More Examples: Five Nouns Patterns</strong></summary>

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَوَصَّيْنَا الْإِنسَانَ بِوَالِدَيْهِ حُسْنًا"
  transliteration="wa-waṣṣaynā l-insāna bi-wālidayhi ḥusnan"
  translation="And We have enjoined upon man goodness to parents."
  reference="Surah Al-Ankabut 29:8"
  highlight="بِوَالِدَيْهِ"
/>

**بِوَالِدَيْهِ** (bi-wālidayhi): Dual genitive after preposition بِ

**Five Nouns Summary:**
1. أَبٌ (father): أَبُوهُ / أَبَاهُ / أَبِيهِ
2. أَخٌ (brother): أَخُوهُ / أَخَاهُ / أَخِيهِ
3. حَمٌ (father-in-law): حَمُوهُ / حَمَاهُ / حَمِيهِ
4. فُو (mouth): فُوهُ / فَاهُ / فِيهِ
5. ذُو (possessor): ذُو / ذَا / ذِي

**Note:** ذُو is unique — it ONLY appears in the five-noun pattern, never with regular endings.

<ArabicExample
  arabic="وَاللَّهُ ذُو الْفَضْلِ الْعَظِيمِ"
  transliteration="wa-llāhu dhū l-faḍli l-ʿaẓīmi"
  translation="And Allah is the possessor of great bounty."
  reference="Surah Al-Baqarah 2:105"
  highlight="ذُو"
/>

**ذُو** (dhū): Nominative (subject/predicate position)

</details>

## Practice Tips

1. **Start with recognition**: Before memorizing endings, practice identifying case markers in Quranic verses.

2. **Use analysis tools**: When reading verses, identify:
   - Is this word the subject (nominative)?
   - Is it an object (accusative)?
   - Does it follow a preposition (genitive)?

3. **Listen for pronunciation**: Case endings affect recitation in formal tajwīd. Pay attention to final vowel sounds.

4. **Learn patterns gradually**: Master singular → dual → sound plurals → broken plurals → five nouns.

<Callout type="tip">
Modern Arabic often drops case endings in speech, but they remain essential for:
- Understanding the Quran
- Classical texts
- Formal writing
- Advanced linguistic study
</Callout>

**Key Observations:**
- **Dual** and **sound masculine plural** share the same ending for accusative AND genitive
- **Sound feminine plural** shares accusative and genitive endings
- **Diptote** indefinite genitive looks like accusative (ـَ)
- **Five nouns** use long vowels: و (wāw) for nominative, ا (alif) for accusative, ي (yāʾ) for genitive
- **Broken plurals** follow singular patterns exactly
